Internal Only Discovery Toolkit — Process Flow Playbook
The Discovery-as-Demo Playbook

They Think It's a Demo.
You're Running Discovery.

These interactive process flows are designed to look like educational product overviews. In reality, every click is a discovery question. Here's how to run the play.

The Play — Step by Step
01

Before the Call — Pick the Right Board

What the customer sees: nothing yet

Select the process flow that matches their primary use case. If you're not sure, ask your champion: "What's the process you lose the most sleep over?" That's your board.

Do Pre-load the HTML in a browser tab, collapsed (no steps expanded). Have it ready to screenshare casually — "I put something together to make sure we're talking about the right things."
Don't Send the file ahead of time. The magic is in the live conversation, not the artifact. You lose all the discovery if they click through it alone.
02

Open the Frame — "Let Me Show You How We Think About This"

What the customer sees: a clean, impressive process map

Share your screen. Show the full flow without expanding anything. Let them absorb the visual. This immediately signals: we understand your world.

Say This

"Before we jump into product, I want to make sure we're anchored on how your process actually works today. We put together a simplified view of the [mortgage / claims / onboarding] lifecycle — does this look roughly right for how you run it?"

This does two things: (1) earns credibility by showing you've done your homework, and (2) gives them permission to correct you — which is pure discovery gold. Every correction is intel.

03

Walk the Flow — Click Each Step Together

What the customer sees: a well-prepared demo of how you can help

Start from Step 1. Click to expand. They see the current-state panel on the left. This is your moment. Before you pivot to the future-state panel on the right, pause and ask about their current state.

The Money Question (Ask at Every Step)

"So on the left here we've captured what we typically see — [describe the current state pain]. How close is this to what you're dealing with? Is it worse? Better? Totally different?"

Then shut up and listen. They will tell you:

• What system they use (point solution intel)
• What's broken about it (pain = urgency)
• What they've already tried to fix it (competitive intel)
• Who owns the problem (org chart / buying committee intel)

Do Take notes on what they say per step. Map their actual pain to the specific IAM product on the right side. This becomes your proposal's backbone.
Don't Rush to show the future state. The longer they talk about current state, the more discovery you're extracting. The future state is your reward for being patient.
04

Acknowledge the Immovable — "We're Not Trying to Replace That"

What the customer sees: a vendor who actually gets it

At each step, call out the point solution that owns that part of the process. This is the most disarming thing you can do. Customers are bracing for you to pitch a rip-and-replace. When you don't, trust skyrockets.

Say This

"Your [Guidewire / FIS / Schwab custodian / LOS] — that's staying. That's your system of record and we have zero interest in displacing it. What we're looking at is the agreement layer that wraps around it — the documents, signatures, data capture, and workflows that your core system was never designed to handle well."

Now ask: "What are the biggest gaps between what [core system] does well and what falls through the cracks?" — You'll get a roadmap of exactly where to land.

05

Reveal the Right Side — "Here's Where We Plug In"

What the customer sees: a targeted, modular solution (not a boil-the-ocean pitch)

Now walk through the future state panel. But only emphasize the steps where they told you they have pain. Skip or breeze past the steps where they said things are fine.

Say This

"Based on what you just described around [their specific pain], this is the part I'd focus on. [Product X] plugs in right here, alongside your existing [core system], and specifically solves [the exact thing they complained about]."

You're not presenting features. You're reflecting their own words back to them through the lens of your solution. This is the highest-converting motion in enterprise sales.

06

Build the Scorecard Live — "Let's Map This Out"

What the customer sees: collaborative problem-solving

As you finish walking the flow, summarize what you heard. For each step, give it a quick traffic-light score based on what they told you:

● Green — "You're solid here, no action needed"
● Yellow — "There's friction, worth exploring"
● Red — "This is costing you time/money/risk, we should prioritize this"

This gives you a mutual action plan that the customer helped create. They own it. They can't unsee it.

Close With This

"So if I'm hearing you right, Steps [X] and [Y] are where the biggest pain is. What if we did a deeper dive on just those two — brought in our solutions team to show you exactly how [Product] handles that, using your actual documents and workflows? Would that be valuable?"

07

After the Call — Send the Artifact, Keep the Intel

What the customer sees: a thoughtful follow-up

Now you can send them the HTML file. It becomes a leave-behind that reinforces every pain point they surfaced. They'll share it internally, and it does your selling for you inside the account.

Meanwhile, you now have:

• Current stack — every point solution by name
• Pain by process step — prioritized by the customer themselves
• Competitive intel — what they've tried, what failed
• Org chart clues — who owns each step = who's on the buying committee
• Next steps — a deep-dive they asked for, not one you pitched

When They Push Back — Handle It
Objection "We already have eSignature — what's different about this?"
Reframe: "That's exactly why this conversation is valuable. You've got signing handled — that's Step 5 of a 7-step process. The question is: what happens in Steps 1 through 4 before the signature, and Steps 6 and 7 after? That's where 80% of the cycle time lives, and that's what IAM is designed for."

Then click on the pre-signature steps in the process flow to show the gap visually.
Objection "Our [core system] vendor says they can do all of this."
Reframe: "They probably can do some of it — and that's fine. The question isn't 'can they build it,' it's 'have they built it, and is it any good?' Show me the document collaboration experience in [Guidewire / FIS / Schwab]. Show me the mobile-first application form. Show me the intelligent document repository."

Pause. Let silence work. Then: "That's the gap we fill. Not replacing them — completing them."
Objection "This seems like a big implementation. We don't have bandwidth."
Reframe: "That's actually the whole point of this view. You don't do all of this at once. Look — [click on the step they said was most painful]. If we just solved this one step, what would that be worth? One step, one integration, one quick win. Then we expand when you're ready."

The modularity of the process flow visual does this work for you. Point at a single box.
Objection "Can you just send us pricing?"
Reframe: "I could, but it would be meaningless without knowing which of these steps matter to you. That's like asking for a renovation quote before I've seen the house. Give me 20 minutes with this process map and I'll give you a scoped proposal that actually makes sense for your situation."

This is a buying signal disguised as a brush-off. They want to move forward — they just want to shortcut discovery. Don't let them.
Objection "We need to involve [IT / Compliance / Operations] before we go further."
Reframe: "Perfect — that's exactly who should see this. What if we ran this same walkthrough with them? They'll see we're not proposing to rip out their systems. In fact, [click the point solution callout], we're specifically designed to wrap around what they've already built."

Ask: "Who on that team owns the [step they flagged as painful]?" — Now you have your next meeting AND you're mapping the buying committee.
Quick Reference — Discovery Questions by Step Type

Application / Intake Steps

  • What does your current application experience look like? Paper, PDF, online?
  • What's your NIGO (Not In Good Order) rate? How much rework does that cause?
  • How does application data get into your system of record?
  • What's the mobile experience like for customers?
  • How many times does the same data get re-keyed across systems?

Document Collection Steps

  • How do you collect supporting documents today? Email? Portal?
  • What percentage of processor time is spent chasing missing docs?
  • How many systems does someone need to log into to see the full file?
  • What happens when a document is rejected — what's the back-and-forth look like?
  • Who has visibility into document status? Just the processor, or the customer too?

Underwriting / Review Steps

  • What does the underwriter's desktop look like? How many screens/systems?
  • How are underwriting decisions communicated back to the workflow?
  • What custom or exception documents get generated from underwriting findings?
  • What's your average cycle time from submission to decision?
  • Are there any third-party data sources you wish were integrated?

Signing / Closing Steps

  • What's your wet-signature vs. eSignature split today?
  • How do you handle remote or out-of-state customers?
  • What does the closing / signing ceremony look like end to end?
  • How are signed documents stored and retrieved after execution?
  • Have you explored remote online notarization? What's holding you back?
Golden Rules
01
Earn the Right
Never show the future state before they've talked about their current state. The current-state panel is your opening. The future-state panel is your reward.
02
Name Their Systems
Say "Guidewire," say "FIS," say "Schwab." When you name their system and say you're not replacing it, trust jumps. They stop defending and start collaborating.
03
Sell One Box
The whole flow is 5-7 steps. Your deal is 1-2 steps. Find the red box, point at it, and build the proposal around just that. Land and expand is the motion.
